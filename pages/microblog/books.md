# Books

## The View from Nowhere, Thomas Nagle (02 JUL -)

Written in the pedantic style of old philosophers. Nagle makes a very big deal about the separation of objective and subjective reality. Basically qualia. He says things that I find silly, like that "being square" is objective, but "being red" is subjective because of how humans percieve colors. I don't get it. From a pure physicalist/realist/reductionist point of view (me!) this is just human sensors diserning a frequency band of electromagnetic radiation. Big deal. "The fealing of red" may be a qualia thing and you may be skeptical of assuming that other humans experience it like you do, etc. But red is just what we humans call a 620-750nm wavelength. What's the big deal? Another silly one is when he presents the argument that existence depends on a mind to percieve. I like Feynman's poem about waves on the beach wasting energy for eons with nothing living to witness them, and the final line about being atoms with conciousness. I do like how Nagle presents all of the top known arguments around each topic. It's far too rigorous for me though. Much of it seems obvious without wasting time pondering things like whether I'm a brain in a vat or the universe is a simulation or whatever else...
